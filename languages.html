Languages of India

There’s a famous aphorism in India. ‘Water changes at every kos, a language changes at every four kos.’ 1 Kos is about 3000 metres. As per ethnologue.com, there are 464 languages in India. The aphorism holds true in certain regions. Still, Hindi, with its wide usage in northern states, is the most spoken language. English follows as a major second language. The provincial or state languages come next. The rest of the languages are all pushed to the margins.

Ethnologue puts these languages on a spectrum where on one end are languages spoken widely and on the other, are languages going extinct.

The four major language families in India are Indo-Aryan, Dravidian, Tibeto-Burman and Austro-Asiatic. In the below tree diagrams, one can trace the languages from their common ancestral language. The term "family" reflects the tree model of language origination in historical linguistics. It is a metaphor comparing languages to people in a biological family tree.

Indo-Aryan Languages

The Indo-Aryan languages are a subgroup of the Indo-European language family. Other Indo-European languages spoken in India include English, French and Portuguese.

Dravidian Languages

The Dravidian languages are a major language family in southern India. Telugu, Tamil, Kannada and Malayalam have the most speakers and also long literary traditions. Smaller literary languages are Tulu and Kodava. There are small groups of Dravidian-speaking tribes, but they live outside Dravidian-speaking areas. Kurukh in Eastern India and Gondi in Central India are a couple of such examples.

Tibeto-Burman Languages

The Tibeto-Burman languages are the non-Sinitic members of the Sino-Tibetan language family. The speakers live across Himalayas, and the north-eastern states. The languages family is so called because it has its origin in either Burmese or Tibetic languages. These languages also have extensive literary traditions dating from the 7th century.

Austro-Asiatic, Andamanese and Other Languages

The Andamanese languages are indigenous, a majority of these have gone extinct. The speakers of Austro-Asiatic live in east and central India, as well as on Nicobar islands. Most of these speakers are tribal communities of Jharkhand, Orissa, Bihar, and West Bengal. Majhwar is an isolated language in Chattisgarh. Kra-dai is the language family of mainland south-east Asia. The speakers of Kra-dai languages live in north-east states.
